## Abstract

Formal methods, in general, are robust structures to filter out errors from widespread usage. For centuries, mathematics has relied on peer review as its main correctness filter, but peer review cannot catch all mistakes and is still subject to the fallible human nature that mathematics often seeks to avoid. In contrast, the newer field of software engineering has accumulated many more layers of mechanical checks: type systems, tests, continuous integration, fuzzing, among others. This report asks what equivalent robustness now exists in mathematics, how these methods demonstrate value, and where they add overhead without a matching gain in rigour. It explores proof assistants and landmark formalisations, then constructs a toy proof assistant to demonstrate its utility with the Deduction Theorem and Glivenko's Theorem. The same theorems are then formalised in Lean 4 for comparison and a glimpse into how the same basic ideas are being extended into the future. Formal methods are most compelling for safety-critical systems, the frontier of research mathematics, and as a structure to ground the overwhelm of AI-assisted proof production.
