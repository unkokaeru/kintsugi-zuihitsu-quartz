# Research with the World's Largest Telescopes

> **Anonymous Code**: 58648
> **Date**: 10th December 2025
> **Speaker**: Dr Katharine Johnston

In 2019, humanity glimpsed the shadow of a black hole, capturing headlines across the world - M87's supermassive black hole went viral, but how was the photo taken? Simply put, by building a telescope the size of the Earth.

The need to keep building larger and larger telescopes is driven by a simple equation, where resolution = λ/D, with λ representing the wavelength of light and D the diameter of the telescope. Hence, a bigger telescope will capture more detail, but there's even more to it - a telescope is basically a giant light bucket. The larger the bucket, the more data collected, in the form of photons, increasing our sensitivity to even the faintest signals. This is why you may have seen those massive radio telescopes, formed of up to 500 meter diameter dishes, easily dwarfing your average astronomy club's telescope.

Despite often thinking that space is empty, it's actually full of dust which becomes very difficult obstacle to overcome for astronomers. Just like fog, this cosmic dust blurs visible data, obscuring the universe beyond it. But some radio wavelengths, just like Wi-Fi, can go through obstacles. As Dr Johnston notes in her lecture, "at wavelengths longer than a micron, you actually don't have any more trouble with dust.", and with this we can transform dust-shrouded regions into a resource to map the thermal/physical properties of clouds of gas and dust.

However, there's a certain manufacturing limit to the size of a telescope. This is where interferometry comes into play. The ALMA - Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array - is 5,000 meters high in Chile's Atacama Desert, with 66 separate antennas working together to create a virtual telescope far larger than a single dish. This breakthrough is possible as resolution doesn't depend on the exact dish size, but on the baseline - the separation between antennas. At these sizes, even the Earth's rotation is useful, allowing the arrays to fill an even larger space as the planet turns. Arrays can talk form all across the world, recording their signals with atomic-clock precision and later correlating them together.

Taking this concept to its extreme, the Event Horizon Telescope (EHT) links radio labs from across the Earth to achieve baselines up to 12,000 kilometres. This Earth-sized telescope can achieve an angular resolution of around 20 microarcseconds; the ability to spot a bottle cap on the Moon. According to the European Space Agency, such precision represents "a resolution sharp enough to read a newspaper in New York from a café in Paris."

These massive telescopes are useful beyond just curiosity, too. They help us understand our cosmic origins, test our fundamental physics, and understand the areas where stars form. As capability increases, more questions can open up about how planets from, how galaxies evolve, and even the nature of spacetime itself.

Sometimes, you just have to build a telescope the size of a planet.
