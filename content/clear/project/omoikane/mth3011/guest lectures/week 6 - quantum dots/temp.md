You’ve probably heard the word ‘quantum’ before and know that it is often used to describe things that are very small and slightly mysterious! More specifically, it refers to systems that are so small that their behaviour is governed by the rules of quantum mechanics rather than classical physics. Quantum dots are a brilliant example of this, as their nanometre-size scale gives rise to unique optical properties.



The seminar ‘Quantum Dots: Applications in Biotechnology’, delivered by Dr Matthew Booth on the 5th of November 2025, explores some of the physics behind quantum dots, their possible applications, and how they can be synthesised.



Dr Booth’s seminar digs deep into the applications in biotechnology, but a firm emphasis is placed that they can be used in a range of things; bioimaging (which we will be exploring), photovoltaic devices (i.e. solar panels), and light emitting devices (like the latest televisions).[1]



Quantum dots are semiconducting crystals on the nanoscale, which essentially just means it’s a crystalline structure halfway between a conductor (like metal) and an insulator (like glass), and its size is typically ~10 nanometres (1 nanometre is one-billionth of a metre).



Inside a semiconductor, electrons can occupy different energy levels. At rest, they sit in the valence band. When given energy, they can jump to the conduction band, which leaves a positively charged ‘hole’ where the electron used to be. The electron and hole form a bound state, which we call an exciton. In quantum dots (QDs), the crystal is sufficiently small such that the exciton is confined to be below its typical size.



This quantum confinement forces the electron to occupy discrete energy levels, rather than the continuous range classically found in semiconductors, which means the colour of light a quantum dot emits can be precisely tuned by altering its size.



QDs can be synthesised in several ways, including solution based “colloidal” methods and growth from substrates. The colloidal method involves controlling the size of the dot by factors like temperature and chemical concentration.



Dr Booth highlighted how these unique properties enable for a range of applications in biotechnology. QDs differ from normal dyes in their emission spectrum; they both emit over a narrow spectrum of wavelengths, but QDs absorb from a wide spectrum of wavelengths, whereas dyes only absorb from a narrow spectrum. This means that we can label different parts of a cell with different QDs, and excite all of them with the same wavelength, but dyes require an individual excitation wavelength for every colour dye used. Furthermore, QDs have much better photostability than dyes, meaning they degrade slower.



One possible use of QDs is screening; they are attached to antibodies which target cancer cells. The cancer cells would take on the QDs, whereas the healthy cells would not. This would be useful for diagnosis or preparing for surgery. [2] There is also the possibility of using QDs to target and heat up cancer cells without affecting healthy cells, which gives rise to the possibility of a non-aggressive cancer treatment.



Although promising, researchers issue caution that the nanomaterials’ toxicity is not yet fully understood, so careful study is needed before proper clinical use. One review of a number of QD studies notes "  
QD absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, and toxicity depend on multiple factors derived from both inherent physicochemical properties and environmental conditions; QD size, charge, concentration, outer coating bioactivity (capping material and functional groups), and oxidative, photolytic, and mechanical stability  
"[3].

Blog report on the Seminar ‘Quantum Dots: Applications in Biotechnology’ by Dr Matthew Booth



There have been many recent advancements in nanotechnology as of late, with one of the most outstanding developments being the emergence of something called ‘Quantum dots’. These tiny structures are crystals on the scale of nanometers that have been formed from semiconductive materials. A material that is semiconductive is more conductive than insulator materials such as rubber, but not quite as conductive as materials that are fully conductive, such as copper, and so possess properties of both. These quantum dots may not sound like much but their unique versatility makes them applicable in a wide variety of areas, such as biotechnology, optical communications, photovoltaics, and even in LEDs and laser development [1]. Dr Matthew Booth highlighted some of their uses in the field of biotechnology in his seminar ‘Quantum Dots: Applications in Biotechnology’ presented on the 5th November 2025, as well as what sets them apart from traditional nanotechnology.



It began when a team at MIT discovered a way to encode information about a person’s vaccination record in the form of a quantum dot tattoo that would lie just under the skin, invisible to the naked eye but able to be scanned by a smartphone or similar device [2]. It had been an ongoing struggle in the medical industry where a patient would go in to get vaccinated but doctors “don’t always know who’s been immunized before and what vaccines are still needed” [2]. By incorporating these quantum dots on a patch of sugar-based microneedles, which dissolve in a matter of minutes, after the vaccine is delivered a pattern is left from the patch just under the patient’s skin. This pattern can be scanned and recognised, similar to a barcode, but using copper-based quantum dots in place of ink.



But how does this work on a quantum scale? Dr Booth suggests using the famous example in quantum physics of visualising a potential well. For those unfamiliar with the example of the potential well, picture a set of axes with two ‘walls’ either side at x=0 and x=L. Inside the walls potential is basically zero. Outside of the walls, where x<0 and x>L, potential is equal to infinity, which means that the probability of finding a particle outside the walls is zero. This is the equivalent of saying that the wavefunction is zero - a general solution function that describes the likelihood of finding a particle at a given point. The wavefunction is also zero at the boundaries of the wall where x=0 and x=L. As the width of the box decreases, the kinetic energy inside the box increases, which means the particles will have more movement. This means that when the size of the crystal is reduced, the energy levels increase and so the band gap - the energy difference between their valance and conduction bands - gets larger. 



One can imagine an incident photon which has an energy that perfectly matches the band gap. When this photon is absorbed by the material, an electron will be promoted from the valence band (the highest occupied energy band) to the conduction band, leaving behind a vacancy. This vacancy has a positive charge relative to its surrounding environment and an effective mass, just like the electron that is now in the conduction band, just with an opposite charge. The process is essentially liberating the electron from the outermost orbitals of an atom such that it can delocalise (move freely) throughout the material. The positively charged vacancy and negatively charged electron interact via the Coulomb interaction and form a bound state. This bound state can be treated like a particle with a Bohr radius, which essentially is the size it wants to exist at. If the crystal is made to be smaller than this radius, strong ‘quantum confinement’ occurs, leading to discrete energy levels. A semiconductor nanocrystal in which charge carriers are confined in all three spatial dimensions is said to be a quantum dot. Since the energy level spacing depends on the size of the dot, the wavelength (colour) of light emitted or absorbed by a quantum dot can be controlled by changing its size.



There are many advantages that come with this type of nanotechnology. Its unique properties such as its narrow emission spectrum, broad absorption spectrum, small size and large surface area, mean that it can be adapted to many uses, can easily be cleared from the body and has a large enough platform to attach things like antibodies to its surface. This means it is particularly useful for diagnosis, surgery, disease detection and more [3]. It is also much more stable than the traditional dyes currently being used, which degrade much quicker. Quantum dot technology can be used to heat up areas locally, which could be vital in treating cancer cells without damaging nearby healthy tissue. However, with the tech being as new as it is, there is always going to be an air of mystery surrounding any long-term side effects, specifically with regards to any forms of nanotoxicity. At such a small scale, nanotoxicity is still an understudied topic and can be much more complicated than it would be at larger sizes, being affected by shape, size and surface chemical composition. 



For now, though, the future of quantum dot technology looks promising as their uses continue to expand horizons within the field of biotechnology and beyond.

In a world where modern media tends to insert the word ‘quantum’ into any unexplainable science-fiction phenomenon, you may assume the term ‘Quantum Dots’ could belong more to a Marvel film than a real-world technology. However these quantum dots are not futuristic inventions pulled from a comic book, but are instead a very real technology used worldwide with applications in everything from solar power, medicine, lasers, chemistry and more![1] Dr Matthew Booth’s seminar titled: “Quantum Dots: Applications in Biotechnology” explores what quantum dots (QDs) are and how they are shaping the modern world. You yourself may even be reading this on a display powered by quantum dots![1][2]

 
What is a ‘quantum dot’ then? Unless you like the answer of ‘quantum dots are semiconductor nanocrystals where the nanocrystal size is smaller than that of its exciton’s Bohr radius’, we’re going to have to tackle some terminology before we can define what these quantum dots actually are.

 
A semiconductor is a material that acts as both an electrical conductor and electrical insulator, meaning it is sometimes great at conducting electricity and sometimes bad at it, depending on how you want to use it. These semiconductors can ‘gain energy’ when a specific light is shone onto them, making the outer electrons that surround the inner nucleus of the semiconductor atoms move outwards, giving these electrons the energy from the light and ‘exciting’ them. We call these excited electrons ‘excitons’, and the distance which they jump is called the ‘Bohr radius’ of the excitons.

 
Now if you create crystals of these semiconductors so small that they are actually smaller than this ‘exciton Bohr radius’ then these tiny semiconducting crystals have special properties; and so we call them ‘Quantum Dots’! These tiny crystals have to be on the nanometer scale (~50,000-100,000x smaller than the width of a human hair) and so we call these nanometer-scale crystals ‘nanocrystals’.

 
Wow, that was a lot! Hopefully now the previous ridiculously scientifically sounding phrase ‘quantum dots are semiconductor nanocrystals where the nanocrystal size is smaller than that of its exciton’s Bohr radius’ actually makes a little bit of sense!

 
So why does Dr Booth care about quantum dots? And why should we? Well quantum dots absorb light of many frequencies (visible light, ultraviolet, etc...), converting them into specific colours depending on the size of their crystals.[2] Thus smaller quantum dots can make blue light and as they get larger, they traverse the colour spectrum to red and beyond. These are then great if you want to create specific light (red, blue, green, etc...) from light of many different colours like white light (which is all the colours combined together).[2]

 
Dr Booth now talks about ‘colloidal’ quantum dots, which are quantum dots that are suspended in a liquid, rather like how fats and proteins are suspended in water to make milk (without chemically reacting with the water). The ‘Solvothermal method’ can be used to create these colloidal quantum dots using Copper-Indium Sulfide crystals as the QDs here. We start with the basic constituents: copper, indium and sulphur in a solution together (mixed in a liquid) which is heated to the point where the atoms form together, forming the crystals, with the longer the length of time that the solution is being heated, resulting in the larger the crystals that form in the solution, which is controllable.

 
So what real-world uses do these quantum dots actually have then?
