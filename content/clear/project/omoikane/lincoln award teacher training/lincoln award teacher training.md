### Reflection 2 - Professional Expectations & Behaviour Management

1. **What are the professional expectations of teachers and in what way are teachers expected to be good role models?**
   Teachers should use a professional persona: dress smartly, be punctual, remain always calm/composed at all times, and use appropriate language around students to avoid political/religious/negative influence. Essentially, I think for a teacher to be a good role model then they're expected to outwardly show dignity, integrity, and general calm - even if inside they're frustrated. Children deserve a trust adult who's consistent and credible.
2. **Why are relationships important when managing behaviour?**
   No-one, especially children, wants to learn from someone they don't like, and it's very difficult to manage someone's behaviour if they don't trust you. What's important is filling a child's "emotional cup" with warmth and genuine interest, then using that positive relationship to redirect their behaviour if needed - hopefully this'll help them respond more positively. This relates a lot to my one-on-one experience too, I think, as building a relationship is incredibly important just so that they don't think each session is a chore and they actually engage.
3. **What strategies may you use to manage behaviour?**
	- Positive praise that's specific and earned.
	- Consistently reinforcing routines, rules (e.g. the school's behaviour policy), and expectations.
	- Using nonverbal communication like eye contact, confident body language, and using strategic silence.
	- Allowing children to have a sense of agency, giving them choices for if they want any help, etc.
	- Deescalating behaviour with calm language, just walking away if needed to give take-up time.
4. **Why should positive behaviour management strategies always be our first recourse?**
   Behaviour management isn't about forcing compliance, it comes from improving the source of any issues; if you create a positive, caring environment where children feel valued (with full emotional cups), then it's much less likely many behaviour issues will arise. Making children feel worse will just end up as a negative cycle.

Then, find your [placement school's behaviour management policy on their website](https://www.prioryacademies.co.uk/_site/data/files/policies/student-welfare/6EEB2A24108F292C80A83B1D9DD7CE28.pdf) - read it carefully and note the language and expectations it sets out. On your first day, observe how your class teacher applies these in practice.

### Reflection 3 - Classroom Pedagogy

1. **How might one of the following impact how teachers deliver teaching and learning in the classroom: working memory, cognitive load, metacognition, retrieval, or long-term memory?**
   Cognitive load seems like one of the biggest factors, to me. Since working memory is so limited, teachers have to be really carefully not to overload students by dumping too much at once. This means teachers should chunk learning into more manageable steps, provide scaffolds and models so make the overall process less overwhelming, and introduce complexity quite gradually; especially if different students move at different paces. I'm sure that, without understanding cognitive load, teachers would be very confused why students aren't retaining any information!
2. **How might one of Barak Rosenshine's Principles of Instruction influence teaching and learning in the classroom?**
   I quite like the principle of beginning each lesson with a review of prior learning, and it reflects how I study with spaced repetition in university quite well. By revisiting previous content at the start of a lesson, you strengthen those neural connections and make retrieval easier over time: something that isn't too hard to implement (a short, fun quiz, for example), but helps significantly overtime. It'll be interesting to see how different teachers implement this is the classroom!
3. **What are five elements considered essential to an effective lesson?**
	- A strong starter to settle the class and set the mood to one of curiosity - with the added bonus of cementing prior learning.
	- Clear modelling by the teacher with explanations of new content in manageable chunks ("I do").
	- Guided practice where the teacher and students can work collaboratively ("We do").
	- Independent practice with relevant scaffolding and appropriate levels of challenge ("You do").
	- A plenary/review that assess understanding and consolidates the lesson.
4. **What strategies might make for a start-to-end effective lesson, and why?**
   Jamie Collier describes an effective lesson as a "three-course meal", beginning with a hook - something visual, questioning, or low-stakes retrieval - to settle and benchmark. Then, the main task following the "I do, we do, you do" model alongside thinking aloud (metacognition) and following up with scaffolded independence; this could include understanding checks throughout too, with mini whiteboards or targeted questioning. Finally, a review the lesson to consolidate everything and prime the students for future lessons. This works by reducing cognitive load, building confidence through success, and supporting the transition from working memory to long-term memory throughout, too.

Then, as you observe lessons in school, look at how the teachers structure teaching and learning: identify the pedagogies, strategies, and research covered in this reflection.
