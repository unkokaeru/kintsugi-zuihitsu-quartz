## 0. Core Definitions & Quick Reference

### Notation Dictionary

| Symbol | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| $S_n$ | Symmetric group on $\{1,\dots,n\}$; all permutations of $n$ elements, $\lvert S_n\rvert = n!$ |
| $A_n$ | Alternating group; even permutations in $S_n$, $\lvert A_n\rvert = n!/2$ |
| $D_{2n}$ | Dihedral group; symmetries of a regular $n$-gon, $\lvert D_{2n}\rvert = 2n$ |
| $C_n$ | Cyclic group of order $n$, generated by a single element |
| $\mathbb{Z}_n$ | Integers modulo $n$ under addition $\oplus$ |
| $\langle g \rangle$ | Cyclic subgroup generated by $g$: $\{e, g, g^2, \dots, g^{o(g)-1}\}$ |
| $\langle S \rangle$ | Subgroup generated by set $S$: smallest subgroup containing all of $S$ |
| $o(g)$ | Order of element $g$: smallest $n \geq 1$ with $g^n = e$ |
| $H \leq G$ | $H$ is a subgroup of $G$ |
| $N \trianglelefteq G$ | $N$ is a normal subgroup of $G$ |
| $G/N$ | Quotient group: cosets of $N$ in $G$ |
| $[G:H]$ | Index of $H$ in $G$: number of distinct cosets |
| $\ker(\theta)$ | Kernel: $\{g \in G : \theta(g) = e_H\}$ |
| $\operatorname{Im}(\theta)$ | Image: $\{\theta(g) : g \in G\}$ |
| $\sigma(g)$ | Signature of permutation $g$: $+1$ (even) or $-1$ (odd) |

### Group Axioms

A **group** $(G, *)$ satisfies: **(1) Closure**: $g*h \in G$. **(2) Identity**: $\exists\, e_G$ with $g * e_G = e_G * g = g$. **(3) Inverses**: $\forall g,\ \exists\, g^{-1}$ with $gg^{-1} = g^{-1}g = e_G$. **(4) Associativity**: $g*(h*k) = (g*h)*k$.

**Key properties**: $(g^{-1})^{-1} = g$; $(gh)^{-1} = h^{-1}g^{-1}$; cancellation: $gh_1 = gh_2 \Rightarrow h_1 = h_2$.

### Quick Subgroup Test (QST)

$H \leq G$ if and only if:

1. **Identity**: $e_G \in H$.
2. **Closure**: $\forall\, h_1, h_2 \in H$, $h_1 h_2 \in H$.
3. **Inverses**: $\forall\, h \in H$, $h^{-1} \in H$.

### Normal Subgroup

$N \trianglelefteq G$ means $N \leq G$ **and** $g^{-1}kg \in N$ for all $k \in N$, $g \in G$.

Equivalently: $gN = Ng$ for all $g \in G$.

### Homomorphism & Isomorphism

- **Homomorphism** $\theta: G \to H$: $\theta(g_1 g_2) = \theta(g_1)\theta(g_2)$ for all $g_1, g_2 \in G$.
- **Isomorphism**: a homomorphism that is also bijective. Write $G \cong H$.
- **Standard consequences**: $\theta(e_G) = e_H$; $\theta(g^m) = (\theta(g))^m$; $\ker(\theta) \trianglelefteq G$; $\operatorname{Im}(\theta) \leq H$.

### Lagrange's Theorem & Corollaries

If $H \leq G$ and $G$ is finite, then $|G| = [G:H] \cdot |H|$, so **$|H|$ divides $|G|$**.

- $[G:H] = |G|/|H|$.
- $o(g)$ divides $|G|$ for any $g \in G$.
- If $|G| = p$ (prime), then $G \cong C_p$ (cyclic).

### Order Switching Lemma

If $N \trianglelefteq G$, then for any $g \in G$ and $k \in N$, there exist $k', k'' \in N$ such that $gk = k'g$ and $kg = gk''$.

### Quotient Group

If $N \trianglelefteq G$, then $G/N = \{gN : g \in G\}$ is a group with operation $(aN)(bN) = (ab)N$. Identity: $e_{G/N} = N$. Inverse: $(aN)^{-1} = a^{-1}N$.

### Permutation Quick Reference

- **Cycle notation**: $(a_1\,a_2\,\dots\,a_r)$ sends $a_1 \to a_2 \to \cdots \to a_r \to a_1$, fixes everything else.
- **Products**: read **right-to-left** (like function composition).
- **Quick Inverse**: reverse each cycle. $(a_1\,a_2\,\dots\,a_r)^{-1} = (a_r\,a_{r-1}\,\dots\,a_1)$. For a product of disjoint cycles, invert each cycle independently.
- **Order**: $o(r\text{-cycle}) = r$. $o(\text{disjoint product}) = \operatorname{lcm}(\text{cycle lengths})$.
- **Disjoint cycles commute**; non-disjoint cycles generally do not.

## 1. Subgroup & Normality Proofs

**Goal**: Prove that $H \leq G$, or that $N \trianglelefteq G$, or related structural properties.

### 1a. Proving $H \leq G$ via Quick Subgroup Test

**Method**: Check the three QST conditions from §0.

1. **Identity**: Show $e_G \in H$ (often by direct calculation or definition).
2. **Closure**: Take arbitrary $h_1, h_2 \in H$ and show $h_1 h_2 \in H$.
3. **Inverses**: Take arbitrary $h \in H$ and show $h^{-1} \in H$.

**Example ($H \cap K \leq G$)**: If $H \leq G$ and $K \leq G$: Identity: $e_G \in H$ and $e_G \in K$, so $e_G \in H \cap K$. Closure: $h, k \in H \cap K \Rightarrow hk \in H$ and $hk \in K$, so $hk \in H \cap K$. Inverses: $h \in H \cap K \Rightarrow h^{-1} \in H$ and $h^{-1} \in K$, so $h^{-1} \in H \cap K$.

**Example ($\operatorname{Im}(\theta) \leq H$)**: For homomorphism $\theta: G \to H$: Identity: $\theta(e_G) = e_H \in \operatorname{Im}(\theta)$. Closure: if $\theta(g_1), \theta(g_2) \in \operatorname{Im}(\theta)$, then $\theta(g_1)\theta(g_2) = \theta(g_1 g_2) \in \operatorname{Im}(\theta)$. Inverses: $\theta(g) \in \operatorname{Im}(\theta) \Rightarrow \theta(g)^{-1} = \theta(g^{-1}) \in \operatorname{Im}(\theta)$.

**Example ($\operatorname{FS}(X) \leq \operatorname{Sym}(X)$)**: For finitary permutations (finite support): Identity: $e$ moves nothing (finite). Closure: $|\operatorname{supp}(fg)| \leq |\operatorname{supp}(f)| + |\operatorname{supp}(g)|$ (finite). Inverses: $\operatorname{supp}(f^{-1}) = \operatorname{supp}(f)$ (finite).

### 1b. Proving $H \leq G$ with Order Switching ($HN \leq G$)

**When**: $H \leq G$, $N \trianglelefteq G$, prove $HN \leq G$.

**Method** (QST + Order Switching):

- **Identity**: $e_G = e_H \cdot e_N \in HN$.
- **Closure**: For $h_1 k_1, h_2 k_2 \in HN$: $(h_1 k_1)(h_2 k_2) = h_1(k_1 h_2)k_2$. By Order Switching, $\exists\, k_1' \in N$ with $k_1 h_2 = h_2 k_1'$. So $(h_1 k_1)(h_2 k_2) = (h_1 h_2)(k_1' k_2) \in HN$.
- **Inverses**: $(hk)^{-1} = k^{-1}h^{-1}$. By Order Switching, $\exists\, k' \in N$ with $k^{-1}h^{-1} = h^{-1}k' \in HN$.

### 1c. Proving Normality

**Method 1 (Direct conjugation)**: Fix arbitrary $g \in G$ and $k \in N$. Show $g^{-1}kg \in N$.

**Method 2 (Kernel of homomorphism)**: If $N = \ker(\theta)$ for some homomorphism $\theta$, then $N \trianglelefteq G$ automatically.

**Method 3 (Index 2)**: If $[G:N] = 2$, then $N \trianglelefteq G$ automatically.

**Example ($H \cap N \trianglelefteq H$)**: For $H \leq G$, $N \trianglelefteq G$: First, $H \cap N \leq H$ by QST. For normality in $H$: fix $h \in H$, $k \in H \cap N$. Then $h^{-1}kh \in H$ (closure in $H$) and $h^{-1}kh \in N$ (normality of $N$ in $G$). So $h^{-1}kh \in H \cap N$.

**Example ($C_3 \trianglelefteq S_3$)**: $S_3 = \{e, (1\,2\,3), (1\,3\,2), (1\,2), (1\,3), (2\,3)\}$, $C_3 = \{e, (1\,2\,3), (1\,3\,2)\}$. For $g \in C_3$: closure handles it. For $g$ a 2-cycle, $c \in C_3$: $g^{-1}cg = gcg$ (since $g^2=e$). Product (2-cycle)(3-cycle) = 2-cycle, then (2-cycle)(2-cycle) = identity or 3-cycle $\in C_3$.

**Example ($HN = NH$)**: $(\subseteq)$ Fix $hk \in HN$. By Order Switching, $\exists\, k' \in N$ with $hk = k'h \in NH$. $(\supseteq)$ Fix $kh \in NH$. By Order Switching, $\exists\, k'' \in N$ with $kh = hk'' \in HN$.

**Example ($\operatorname{FS}(X) \trianglelefteq \operatorname{Sym}(X)$)**: For $g \in \operatorname{Sym}(X)$, $h \in \operatorname{FS}(X)$ with $\operatorname{supp}(h) = S$ (finite): $g^{-1}hg$ fixes all $x \notin g^{-1}(S)$, and $|g^{-1}(S)| = |S|$ (finite), so $g^{-1}hg \in \operatorname{FS}(X)$.

## 2. Homomorphism & Isomorphism Proofs

**Goal**: Verify a map is a homomorphism/isomorphism, prove standard properties, apply isomorphism theorems.

### 2a. Verifying a Homomorphism

**Method**: Show $\theta(g_1 g_2) = \theta(g_1)\theta(g_2)$ for all $g_1, g_2 \in G$.

- For maps defined on generators: check the defining relations are preserved.
- For maps defined by formula: compute both sides and show equality.

**Example ($\varphi: H \to (HN)/N$, $\varphi(h) = hN$)**: $\varphi(h_1 h_2) = (h_1 h_2)N = (h_1 N)(h_2 N) = \varphi(h_1)\varphi(h_2)$.

### 2b. Disproving a Homomorphism

**Method**: Find a single counterexample where $\theta(ab) \neq \theta(a)\theta(b)$.

**Example (Squaring $\varphi(g) = g^2$ on non-Abelian groups)**: In $S_7$: let $a = (1\,2)$, $b = (2\,3)$. Then $\varphi(a)\varphi(b) = e \cdot e = e$, but $\varphi(ab) = ((1\,2)(2\,3))^2 = (1\,2\,3)^2 = (1\,3\,2) \neq e$.

*Note*: On Abelian groups, $\varphi(g) = g^2$ is always a homomorphism since $(ab)^2 = a^2 b^2$.

### 2c. Proving $\theta(e_G) = e_H$

**Proof**: $\theta(g) = \theta(e_G \cdot g) = \theta(e_G)\theta(g)$. Right-multiply by $\theta(g)^{-1}$: $e_H = \theta(e_G)$.

### 2d. Proving $\theta(g^m) = (\theta(g))^m$

**Proof by induction**: Base: $\theta(g^1) = \theta(g)$. Step: $\theta(g^{m+1}) = \theta(g^m \cdot g) = \theta(g^m)\theta(g) = (\theta(g))^m \theta(g) = (\theta(g))^{m+1}$.

### 2e. Proving $\ker(\theta) \trianglelefteq G$

**Proof**: First, $\ker(\theta) \leq G$ by QST: $\theta(e_G) = e_H$; $\theta(ab) = e_H e_H = e_H$; $\theta(a^{-1}) = \theta(a)^{-1} = e_H$.

For normality: fix $g \in G$, $k \in \ker(\theta)$: $\theta(g^{-1}kg) = \theta(g)^{-1}\theta(k)\theta(g) = \theta(g)^{-1} e_H \theta(g) = e_H$, so $g^{-1}kg \in \ker(\theta)$.

### 2f. Constructing an Isomorphism $G \cong H$

**Method**:

1. Define a map $\theta: G \to H$ (usually by specifying images of generators).
2. Check the defining relations are preserved (this gives homomorphism).
3. Show **surjectivity**: compute images of all elements and verify they cover $H$.
4. Show **injectivity**: either $|G| = |H|$ (with surjectivity), or show $\ker(\theta) = \{e\}$.

**Example ($D_6 \cong S_3$)**: Define $\theta(\rho) = (1\,2\,3)$, $\theta(\sigma) = (1\,2)$. Check: $\theta(\rho)^3 = e$ ✓, $\theta(\sigma)^2 = e$ ✓, $\theta(\rho)\theta(\sigma) = \theta(\sigma)\theta(\rho)^{-1}$ ✓. Extend: $\theta(\rho^i) = (1\,2\,3)^i$, $\theta(\sigma\rho^i) = (1\,2)(1\,2\,3)^i$. All 6 images are distinct elements of $S_3$, so $\theta$ is bijective.

### 2g. Isomorphism Theorems

**First Isomorphism Theorem (FIT)**: If $\theta: G \to H$ is a homomorphism, then $G/\ker(\theta) \cong \operatorname{Im}(\theta)$.

*Application*: To show $G/N \cong H$, find a surjective homomorphism $\theta: G \to H$ with $\ker(\theta) = N$.

**Second Isomorphism Theorem**: $H \leq G$, $N \trianglelefteq G$ $\Rightarrow$ $H/(H \cap N) \cong (HN)/N$.

*Proof via FIT*: Map $\varphi: H \to (HN)/N$ by $\varphi(h) = hN$. Kernel: $H \cap N$. Image: $(HN)/N$. Apply FIT.

**Third Isomorphism Theorem** ("Fool's Cancellation"): $N \trianglelefteq G$, $N \leq M \trianglelefteq G$ $\Rightarrow$ $(G/N)/(M/N) \cong G/M$.

*Proof via FIT*: Map $\varphi: G/N \to G/M$ by $\varphi(gN) = gM$. Well-defined since $N \leq M$. Kernel: $M/N$. Image: $G/M$. Apply FIT.

**Example**: $\operatorname{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})/\operatorname{SL}_2(\mathbb{R})\ \big/\ P/\operatorname{SL}_2(\mathbb{R}) \cong \operatorname{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})/P$. Here $\operatorname{SL}_2(\mathbb{R}) \leq P \trianglelefteq \operatorname{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})$, both normal (via $\det$ homomorphism). $\operatorname{GL}_2/\operatorname{SL}_2 \cong (\mathbb{R}^*, \times)$ (cosets indexed by determinant), $P/\operatorname{SL}_2 \cong (\mathbb{R}_{>0}, \times)$ (positive determinants), $\operatorname{GL}_2/P \cong C_2$ (sign of determinant).

### 2h. Proving Normality via Homomorphisms

To show $N \trianglelefteq G$: find a homomorphism $\theta: G \to H$ such that $N = \ker(\theta)$.

**Example ($P \trianglelefteq \operatorname{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ and $\operatorname{SL}_2(\mathbb{R}) \trianglelefteq \operatorname{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})$)**: The determinant $\det: \operatorname{GL}_2(\mathbb{R}) \to (\mathbb{R}^*, \times)$ is a homomorphism (since $\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$ and $\det(A^{-1}) = \det(A)^{-1}$). Then $\operatorname{SL}_2(\mathbb{R}) = \ker(\det) \trianglelefteq \operatorname{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})$. For $P = \{M : \det(M) > 0\}$: compose $\det$ with the sign function $\operatorname{sgn}: \mathbb{R}^* \to \{1, -1\}$ (which is itself a homomorphism since $\operatorname{sgn}(ab) = \operatorname{sgn}(a)\operatorname{sgn}(b)$). The composition $\operatorname{sgn} \circ \det: \operatorname{GL}_2(\mathbb{R}) \to \{1, -1\}$ is a homomorphism (composition of homomorphisms is a homomorphism), with $P = \ker(\operatorname{sgn} \circ \det) \trianglelefteq \operatorname{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})$.

**Quotient descriptions**: $\operatorname{GL}_2/\operatorname{SL}_2 \cong (\mathbb{R}^*, \times)$ (by FIT applied to $\det$; cosets indexed by determinant value). $P/\operatorname{SL}_2 \cong (\mathbb{R}_{>0}, \times)$ (positive determinants only). $\operatorname{GL}_2/P \cong C_2$ (sign of determinant: $\{+, -\}$).

**Example ($S_n/A_n \cong C_2$ via signature)**: The signature $\sigma: S_n \to \{1, -1\}$ is a surjective homomorphism with $\ker(\sigma) = A_n$. By FIT: $S_n/A_n \cong \{1, -1\} \cong C_2$.

## 3. Signatures & Alternating Group

**Goal**: Compute signatures, determine $A_n$ membership, list elements of $A_n$.

### 3a. Signature Computation

**Formula**: For a permutation $g = c_1 c_2 \cdots c_m$ (disjoint cycles of lengths $r_1, r_2, \dots, r_m$):

$$
\sigma(g) = (-1)^{r_1 - 1} \cdot (-1)^{r_2 - 1} \cdots (-1)^{r_m - 1} = (-1)^{(r_1-1) + (r_2-1) + \cdots + (r_m-1)}
$$

**Quick rules**:

| Cycle length $r$ | $\sigma = (-1)^{r-1}$ | Even/Odd |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 (fixed point) | $+1$ | Even |
| 2 (transposition) | $-1$ | Odd |
| 3 | $+1$ | Even |
| 4 | $-1$ | Odd |
| $r$ odd | $+1$ | Even |
| $r$ even | $-1$ | Odd |

**Shortcut**: Count the number of even-length cycles. If that count is even, $\sigma(g) = +1$; if odd, $\sigma(g) = -1$.

### 3b. Signature Properties

- $\sigma$ is a homomorphism: $\sigma(gh) = \sigma(g)\sigma(h)$.
- $\sigma(g^{-1}) = \sigma(g)$ (inverse has same cycle shape).
- Signature depends only on the **cycle shape** (tuple of cycle lengths).

**Using the homomorphism property** (avoids recomputing cycle decompositions):

- $\sigma(gh) = \sigma(g)\sigma(h)$ — multiply signatures instead of decomposing the product.
- $\sigma(g^{-1}) = \sigma(g)$ — same parity as $g$.
- $\sigma(g^{-1}hg) = \sigma(g^{-1})\sigma(h)\sigma(g) = \sigma(g)\sigma(h)\sigma(g) = \sigma(h)$ — conjugation preserves signature.

**Example**: $g = (6\,1\,4)(5\,3\,2\,7)(8\,9\,10)$, $h = (1\,2)(3\,4)(5\,6)(7\,8)$. Signatures: $\sigma(g) = (-1)^2(-1)^3(-1)^2 = -1$ (odd), $\sigma(h) = (-1)^1(-1)^1(-1)^1(-1)^1 = +1$ (even). Then: $\sigma(g^{-1}) = \sigma(g) = -1$; $\sigma(gh) = (-1)(+1) = -1$; $\sigma(g^{-1}hg) = \sigma(h) = +1$.

### 3c. Alternating Group $A_n$

**Definition**: $A_n = \ker(\sigma) = \{g \in S_n : \sigma(g) = +1\}$ (even permutations).

**Key facts**: $|A_n| = n!/2$; $A_n \trianglelefteq S_n$; $A_n$ is simple for $n \geq 5$.

### 3d. Listing All Cycle Shapes in $S_n$ and Identifying $A_n$ Elements

**Method**:

1. List all **partitions** of $n$ (ways to write $n$ as a sum of positive integers in non-increasing order). Each partition gives a cycle shape (including 1-cycles).
2. For each cycle shape, compute the signature.
3. Those with signature $+1$ are in $A_n$.

**Example ($S_5$ cycle shapes)**:

| Cycle shape | Example | $\sigma$ | In $A_5$? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $(1,1,1,1,1)$ | $e$ | $+1$ | ✓ |
| $(2,1,1,1)$ | $(1\,2)$ | $-1$ | ✗ |
| $(2,2,1)$ | $(1\,2)(3\,4)$ | $+1$ | ✓ |
| $(3,1,1)$ | $(1\,2\,3)$ | $+1$ | ✓ |
| $(3,2)$ | $(1\,2\,3)(4\,5)$ | $-1$ | ✗ |
| $(4,1)$ | $(1\,2\,3\,4)$ | $-1$ | ✗ |
| $(5)$ | $(1\,2\,3\,4\,5)$ | $+1$ | ✓ |

### 3e. Counting Permutations with a Given Cycle Shape

When listing all permutations with a given shape, be careful not to double-count cyclic rotations: e.g. $(1\,2\,3) = (2\,3\,1) = (3\,1\,2)$.

**Number of $r$-cycles in $S_n$**: $\binom{n}{r} \cdot (r-1)!$. For example, 3-cycles in $S_5$: $\binom{5}{3} \cdot 2! = 20$.

**Verify**: Total across all cycle shapes should equal $n!$.

## 4. Permutation Computation

**Goal**: Convert between notations, compute products/inverses, list cyclic group elements, find symmetry groups.

### 4a. Converting Bijection Notation ↔ Cycle Notation

**Bijection → Cycles**: Start at 1, follow the arrows until you return. Write that cycle. Move to the next unvisited number and repeat.

**Cycles → Bijection**: For each number, trace through the cycles to find its image.

### 4b. Computing Products of Permutations

**Method** (right-to-left): To compute $\alpha\beta$, for each element $x$: first apply $\beta$ (rightmost), then apply $\alpha$.

1. Start with the smallest element $x$.
2. Feed $x$ through $\beta$ to get $\beta(x)$, then through $\alpha$ to get $\alpha(\beta(x))$.
3. Record $x \mapsto \alpha(\beta(x))$ and continue from $\alpha(\beta(x))$ until a cycle closes.
4. Move to the next unvisited element.

### 4c. Computing Inverses

**Quick Inverse Proposition**: Reverse the order of elements within each cycle.

- $(a\,b\,c\,d)^{-1} = (d\,c\,b\,a)$.
- $(c_1 c_2 \cdots c_m)^{-1} = c_1^{-1} c_2^{-1} \cdots c_m^{-1}$ (invert each disjoint cycle independently).

**Verify**: $gg^{-1}$ should give the identity.

### 4d. Listing Elements of $\langle G \rangle$

**Method**: Compute $g^0 = e,\ g^1,\ g^2,\ \dots$ until you get $e$ again. The order is $o(g) = \operatorname{lcm}(\text{cycle lengths})$.

**Example**: $g = (1\,3\,5\,7)(2\,4) \in S_{10}$, $o(g) = \operatorname{lcm}(4,2) = 4$. So $\langle g \rangle = \{e, g, g^2, g^3\}$. Compute each power by composing $g$ with itself.

### 4e. Symmetry Groups of Shapes

**Method**: Identify all rigid motions (rotations and reflections) that map the shape to itself.

1. Label corners.
2. Find all rotations: if the shape has $n$-fold rotational symmetry, $\rho = (1\,2\,\dots\,n)(\text{etc.})$.
3. Find all reflections: each line of symmetry gives a product of transpositions.
4. Write each symmetry in cycle notation.

## 5. Group Algebraic Proofs

**Goal**: Prove identities like $(g^{-1}hg)^n = g^{-1}h^n g$, show groups are cyclic, prove $|\langle g\rangle| = o(g)$.

### 5a. Telescoping / Cancellation Pattern

**Key trick**: Expand a power and cancel adjacent $gg^{-1} = e$ pairs.

**Example** ($(g^{-1}hg)^n = g^{-1}h^n g$): Expand: $(g^{-1}hg)(g^{-1}hg)\cdots(g^{-1}hg) = g^{-1}h\underbrace{(gg^{-1})}_{e}h\underbrace{(gg^{-1})}_{e}\cdots hg = g^{-1}h^n g$.

### 5b. Proof by Induction

**Structure**: Base case ($n = 1$), inductive step (assume for $n$, prove for $n+1$).

**Example** ($\theta(g^m) = (\theta(g))^m$): Base: $\theta(g^1) = \theta(g)$. Step: $\theta(g^{m+1}) = \theta(g^m \cdot g) = \theta(g^m)\theta(g) = (\theta(g))^m \theta(g) = (\theta(g))^{m+1}$.

### 5c. Proving $|\langle G \rangle| = o(g)$

Let $n = o(g)$. Then $\{g^0, g^1, \dots, g^{n-1}\} \subseteq \langle g \rangle$, so $|\langle g \rangle| \geq n$. For any $g^m \in \langle g \rangle$, write $m = qn + r$ with $0 \leq r < n$: $g^m = (g^n)^q g^r = g^r \in \{g^0, \dots, g^{n-1}\}$. So $|\langle g \rangle| \leq n$, giving $|\langle g \rangle| = n = o(g)$.

### 5d. Proving a Group is Cyclic

**Method**: Find a single element $g$ such that $\langle g \rangle = G$.

**Example** ($\mathbb{Z}_n = \langle [1]_n \rangle$): Any $[m]_n \in \mathbb{Z}_n$ satisfies $[m]_n = \underbrace{[1]_n \oplus \cdots \oplus [1]_n}_{m} = ([1]_n)^m \in \langle [1]_n \rangle$.

### 5e. Proving $\langle \rho, \sigma \rangle = D_{2n}$

**Method**: Show every element of $D_{2n}$ can be written using only $\rho$ and $\sigma$:

1. Any product of $\rho$'s and $\sigma$'s can be reduced to $\sigma^k \rho^n$ using $\rho\sigma = \sigma\rho^{-1}$ (commute all $\sigma$'s to the left, inverting $\rho$'s as they pass).
2. Since $\sigma^2 = e$ and $\rho^n = e$, only $\sigma^0$ or $\sigma^1$ and $\rho^0, \dots, \rho^{n-1}$ survive.
3. This gives $2n$ elements $= |D_{2n}|$, so $\langle \rho, \sigma \rangle = D_{2n}$.

## 6. Solving Permutation Equations

**Goal**: Find $\sigma$ satisfying equations like $\alpha\sigma\beta = \gamma$.

### Method

**Isolate $\sigma$** by multiplying by inverses:

| Equation | Solution |
| :--- | :--- |
| $\alpha\sigma = \gamma$ | $\sigma = \alpha^{-1}\gamma$ |
| $\sigma\beta = \gamma$ | $\sigma = \gamma\beta^{-1}$ |
| $\alpha\sigma\beta = \gamma$ | $\sigma = \alpha^{-1}\gamma\beta^{-1}$ |

**Steps**:

1. If $\sigma$ is left-multiplied by $\alpha$: left-multiply both sides by $\alpha^{-1}$.
2. If $\sigma$ is right-multiplied by $\beta$: right-multiply both sides by $\beta^{-1}$.
3. Compute the inverse(s) using the Quick Inverse Proposition.
4. Multiply out the resulting permutations (right-to-left) to get $\sigma$ as disjoint cycles.
5. **Verify** by substituting back.

**Example**: $(1\,2\,3)(4\,5\,6\,7\,8) = (1\,5\,7\,8)\sigma(1\,3\,4)$

- Right-multiply by $(1\,3\,4)^{-1} = (1\,4\,3)$: $(1\,2\,3)(4\,5\,6\,7\,8)(1\,4\,3) = (1\,5\,7\,8)\sigma$.
- Left-multiply by $(1\,5\,7\,8)^{-1} = (8\,7\,5\,1)$: $\sigma = (8\,7\,5\,1)(1\,2\,3)(4\,5\,6\,7\,8)(1\,4\,3) = (2\,3)(4\,8)(5\,6)$.

## 7. Dihedral Group Structure

**Goal**: List elements of $D_{2n}$, write in cycle notation, prove identities, construct shapes.

### 7a. Standard Form of $D_{2n}$

$$
D_{2n} = \{e, \rho, \rho^2, \dots, \rho^{n-1}, \sigma, \sigma\rho, \sigma\rho^2, \dots, \sigma\rho^{n-1}\}
$$

where $\rho$ is anticlockwise rotation by $2\pi/n$ and $\sigma$ is a reflection.

**Defining relations**: $\rho^n = e$, $\sigma^2 = e$, $\rho\sigma = \sigma\rho^{-1} = \sigma\rho^{n-1}$.

- Rotations: $\rho^i$ (even permutations).
- Reflections: $\sigma\rho^i$ (odd permutations, each has order 2).

### 7b. Writing $D_{2n}$ in Cycle Notation

Label corners $1, 2, \dots, n$ anticlockwise. Then:

- $\rho = (1\,2\,3\,\dots\,n)$.
- $\sigma$ = reflection through the line passing through corner 1: $\sigma = (2\,n)(3\,n{-}1)(4\,n{-}2)\cdots$

Compute $\rho^k$ by composing $\rho$ with itself $k$ times. Compute $\sigma\rho^k$ by multiplying.

### 7c. Proving $\rho\sigma = \sigma\rho^{-1}$

$\rho\sigma$ is a reflection (product of rotation and reflection), so $o(\rho\sigma) = 2$, meaning $(\rho\sigma)^2 = e$, i.e. $\rho\sigma\rho\sigma = e$. Left-multiply by $\rho^{-1}\sigma^{-1} = \rho^{-1}\sigma$ (since $\sigma^2 = e$): $\rho\sigma = \sigma\rho^{-1}$.

Since $\rho^{-1} = \rho^{n-1}$: $\rho\sigma = \sigma\rho^{n-1}$.

### 7d. Constructing a Shape with $D_{2n}$ Symmetry

To make a shape (not a regular $n$-gon) with $D_{2n}$ symmetry: start with a regular $n$-gon and attach identical decorations (e.g., a small square on each edge, or a bump on each vertex) preserving all $n$ rotational and $n$ reflective symmetries.

## 8. Lagrange & Orders

**Goal**: Use Lagrange's Theorem for nonexistence proofs, classify groups of prime order, compute orders.

### 8a. Nonexistence of Subgroups

**Method**: If $|H|$ does not divide $|G|$, then no such subgroup $H$ exists.

**Example**: $S_5$ has no subgroup of order 9, because $|S_5| = 120$ and $9 \nmid 120$.

### 8b. Prime Order $\Rightarrow$ Cyclic

**Theorem**: If $|G| = p$ (prime), then $G$ is cyclic and $G \cong C_p$.

**Proof**: Take non-identity $g \in G$. Then $\langle g \rangle \leq G$ with $|\langle g \rangle|$ dividing $p$. Since $g \neq e$, $|\langle g \rangle| \neq 1$, so $|\langle g \rangle| = p = |G|$, hence $\langle g \rangle = G$.

**Corollary**: The only subgroups of $G$ (with $|G| = p$) are $\{e\}$ and $G$.

### 8c. Orders of Permutations

**Formula**: $o(g) = \operatorname{lcm}(r_1, r_2, \dots, r_m)$ where $r_i$ are the disjoint cycle lengths.

**Finding an element of order $k$ in $S_n$**: Write $k$ as a product of prime powers, and choose disjoint cycles whose lengths have lcm equal to $k$, with total length $\leq n$.

**Example**: Element of order 15 in $S_{10}$: $15 = \operatorname{lcm}(5, 3)$, so $(1\,2\,3\,4\,5)(6\,7\,8) \in S_{10}$ has order 15 (total length $5 + 3 = 8 \leq 10$).

**Nonexistence**: No element of order 19 in $S_{10}$, because 19 is prime so we'd need a 19-cycle, but $19 > 10$.

### 8d. Useful Consequences

- $o(g)$ divides $|G|$ for any $g$ in a finite group $G$.
- $g^{|G|} = e$ for any $g$ in a finite group $G$.
- $[G:H] = 2 \Rightarrow H \trianglelefteq G$.
